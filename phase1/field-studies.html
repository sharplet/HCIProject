Overview and interview guide

We opted to do in-person interviews. Each of us separately questioned two or three people for a total of 9 interviews. We also developed a list of questions to guide our interviews, though not all interviews included the full list, and neither does the list represent everything we finally discussed.

This is the question guide we used:

  • Do you see any problems with textbooks in their current form? (Too heavy, too big? …)
  • Alternately, what frustrates you about ebooks?
  • Have you ever read an ebook?
  • What do you like/dislike about ebooks?
  • What is your opinion on PDF vs. kindle vs. ipad vs. sony, etc.
  • Do you think a textbook would work as an ebook? What would be right/wrong with this?
  • Do you like to read on the internet? Laptop? Desktop? Phone?
  • What is the most important/best thing about paper books?
  • Do you read textbooks front to back, as reference books, or both?
  • Estimate of how much time spent reading books? (vs. internet?)
  • How much time do you spend reading in one sitting?
  • Does reading on a screen hurt your eyes?
  • What helps you to read more, or read for longer periods of time? (Things to help concentrate, etc.)
  • What time of the day do you like to read?

Rationale

Although there is a sizable number of people who have strong opinions on textbooks, we preferred to use personal interviews instead of surveys. We decided we wanted to use interviews to explore our interviewees’ opinions, rather than guess at all the relevant issues and prepare a fixed set of questions.

Summary

The two most crucial considerations were:

  • The bulkiness, weight and heat of laptop (or computer) make reading actual books preferable
  • Price: iPads are too expensive, and real textbooks are too expensive

Digital textbooks will be desirable if they are cheap, light and easy to navigate.
